# 4.1 Purpose and Philosophy

## 4.1.1 Purpose

The Farm Knowledge Model is the intellectual foundation of FarmOS.

Traditional farm software usually stores records. FarmOS must go further. It must transform daily farm observations into structured knowledge, explainable recommendations, and better decisions.

This section defines why FarmOS is designed as a knowledge system rather than a simple record management system.

## 4.1.2 Problem Statement

Modern farms generate information every day:

- milk production
- egg production
- feed distribution
- animal observations
- veterinary treatment
- weight records
- crop observations
- harvest records
- inventory changes
- sales
- expenses
- weather conditions

Most systems store these records separately. As a result:

- observations remain isolated
- trends are missed
- decisions rely heavily on memory
- worker feedback can become subjective
- knowledge disappears when experienced workers leave
- early warning signs are missed

FarmOS solves this by treating every observation as part of a growing knowledge base.

## 4.1.3 Core Philosophy

FarmOS operates under the following philosophy:

> FarmOS does not replace the farmer's experience. It amplifies it.

The worker captures reality.  
FarmOS correlates information.  
The farm manager decides.  
The veterinarian diagnoses.  
The outcome becomes institutional memory.

## 4.1.4 System of Understanding

FarmOS is not only a system of record.

A system of record answers:

> What happened?

FarmOS must answer:

> What does it mean, what should we watch, and what decision should the farm manager consider?

Example:

A worker records:

- Cow 744 ate less than usual.
- Cow 744 produced 18% less milk.
- Cow 744 had elevated temperature.
- Cow 744 had mastitis history.

FarmOS should not diagnose mastitis.  
FarmOS should generate an explainable health recommendation:

> Cow 744 requires veterinary review because multiple indicators suggest abnormal health status.

## 4.1.5 Human Roles

| Actor | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| Worker | Records objective observations |
| Farm Manager | Makes operational and financial decisions |
| Veterinarian | Diagnoses and prescribes treatment |
| FarmOS | Correlates evidence and generates recommendations |
| AI | Supports reasoning but never replaces human authority |

## 4.1.6 Design Rules

### RULE-KM-101 — Evidence Before Recommendation

Every recommendation must be supported by evidence.

### RULE-KM-102 — No Hidden Reasoning

Every recommendation must explain why it was generated.

### RULE-KM-103 — Missing Data Reduces Confidence

Missing observations must reduce confidence rather than be ignored.

### RULE-KM-104 — Workers Do Not Diagnose

Workers record objective observations. They do not select diagnoses.

### RULE-KM-105 — Outcomes Become Learning

Every decision outcome must improve future recommendations.

## 4.1.8 Codex Implementation Notes

When implementing FarmOS:

- Do not build AI features as a separate chatbot first.
- Build the observation and recommendation data structures first.
- All AI outputs must reference evidence.
- All recommendations must include confidence.
- Do not allow workers to enter medical diagnoses as routine observations.
- Ensure all knowledge objects are traceable to observations.

## 4.1.9 Acceptance Criteria

This section is satisfied when:

- FarmOS distinguishes observations, knowledge, recommendations, decisions, outcomes, and learning.
- Recommendations cannot exist without evidence.
- AI outputs are explainable.
- Human authority is preserved.
- Worker input is structured around observations rather than opinions.
